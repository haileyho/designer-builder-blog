Why Every Good Design Starts with a Bad Sketch

By Hailey Ho June 17, 2026 8 min read

The blank screen is the enemy of creativity. There's something uniquely paralyzing about an empty Figma canvas or a blank Sketch artboard staring back at you, cursor blinking, waiting. It demands perfection before you've even begun.

The solution isn't better software or a more sophisticated design system. It's a piece of paper and the worst pen you own.

The Permission Problem

Most designers — especially early in their careers — treat the design process as a single, continuous act. You open your tool, you design, you ship. But that's not how creativity works. Great design is actually two very different cognitive activities collapsed into one uncomfortable experience: exploring and refining.

Exploring requires permission to be wrong. Lots of things. Sketches that will never see daylight, layouts that go nowhere, ideas you'd be embarrassed to show anyone. The roughness of pencil on paper gives your brain that permission. There's no undo, no grid snapping, no perfectly rendered shadow. It's just marks, fast and loose.

"The first idea is never the right idea. But it's the only way to get to the right idea."

What Actually Happens When You Sketch First

When you reach for a pen before opening your design tool, a few interesting things happen. First, you move faster. A sketch takes thirty seconds. A wireframe takes thirty minutes. In the time it takes to set up a responsive grid in Figma, you can explore fifteen different layout ideas on paper.

Second, you make different decisions. The very act of using a low-fidelity medium forces you to focus on structure and hierarchy rather than color and decoration. You can't kern type on paper. You can't tweak a shadow radius. You're left with only what matters: does this layout communicate the right thing? Is this the right amount of content for this page?

Third — and this one surprised me when I first noticed it — you become less precious about your ideas. It's easy to fall in love with a Figma component you spent an hour perfecting. It's much harder to get attached to a box you drew in thirty seconds. Bad sketches make for better design decisions because you haven't invested enough to feel the loss of abandoning them.

How I Do It

My process is embarrassingly simple. Before any new project or feature, I give myself exactly fifteen minutes with an A5 notebook and a felt-tip pen. The pen matters — it bleeds, it can't be erased, and it commits you to decisions. I draw quick thumbnails of layouts, maybe four or six per page. I label them, circle the promising parts, and cross out the rest.

Only after I've filled at least one page do I open my design tool. By that point, I already know what I want to build. The software becomes what it should always be: a tool for refinement, not exploration.

The Myth of the Clean Process

There's a pervasive idea in design culture — fed by polished portfolio case studies and tool tutorials — that good designers work in clean, linear ways. Discovery flows neatly into definition, which flows into ideation, which flows into delivery. The reality is messier and more honest: it starts with bad ideas, and the good ones emerge from the wreckage.

Embrace the bad sketch. It's not a sign that you don't know what you're doing. It's a sign that you're doing the real work.
